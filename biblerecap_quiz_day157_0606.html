QUESTION 1 OF 10
What does Proverbs 10:12 say love does, in contrast to hatred which stirs up strife?
PROVERBS 10:12
"Hatred stirs up strife, but love covers all offenses." The verb 'covers' does real theological work here. Love does not erase the offense or pretend it did not happen; it absorbs it so that it cannot metastasize into ongoing conflict. The apostle Peter cites this proverb directly (1 Pet. 4:8), applying it to Christian community: those who know their own sins covered by Christ's atoning love have the resource to cover the offenses of others.
QUESTION 2 OF 10 DETAIL
According to Proverbs 10:26, what two things is a sluggard compared to when sent on an errand?
PROVERBS 10:26
"Like vinegar to the teeth and smoke to the eyes, so is a sluggard to those who send him." Both images describe involuntary, physical irritation — the kind that cannot be willed away. The sluggard's failure is not merely disappointing; it is experienced as a kind of pain by those who depended on him. The proverb lets the imagery do the work without moralizing: place the sluggard in front of your imagination and the point is felt before it is argued.
QUESTION 3 OF 10
How does Proverbs 11:1 describe a false balance in the LORD's sight?
PROVERBS 11:1
"A false balance is an abomination to the LORD, but a just weight is his delight." The word 'abomination' (Hebrew: to'evah) is the same term used to describe idolatry — the strongest available expression of divine revulsion. In the ancient market, merchants routinely carried two sets of weights: one for buying (heavier), one for selling (lighter). The LORD's naming of this practice an abomination signals that economic dishonesty is not a minor social failing but an offense against his own character as the God of truth and justice.
QUESTION 4 OF 10 DETAIL
Proverbs 11:22 uses a striking image to describe a beautiful woman who lacks discretion. What is it?
PROVERBS 11:22
"Like a gold ring in a pig's snout is a beautiful woman who shows no discretion." The image is deliberately jarring: a gold ring is genuinely precious, but it has been placed where it will be defiled rather than honored. The point is not contempt for beauty but a statement about the relationship between beauty and character. Discretion (Hebrew: ta'am — taste, judgment, sensibility) provides beauty its proper setting; without it, outward loveliness is actively misplaced.
QUESTION 5 OF 10
According to Proverbs 11:24, what happens to the one who gives freely?
PROVERBS 11:24–25
"One person gives freely, yet gains even more; another withholds unduly, but comes to poverty." The paradox inverts the logic of conservation: the hoarder loses, the giver gains. This is not a mechanical prosperity promise but a description of how the LORD's moral economy operates. Generosity participates in a flow of blessing; stinginess cuts the giver off from it. Verse 25 confirms: "A generous person will prosper; whoever refreshes others will be refreshed."
QUESTION 6 OF 10
Proverbs 12:1 calls the person who hates correction by a particular word. What is it?
PROVERBS 12:1
"Whoever loves discipline loves knowledge, but whoever hates correction is stupid." The word translated 'stupid' (Hebrew: ba'ar — brutish, beast-like) is blunter than the usual vocabulary for folly; it is the word applied to unreasoning animals. The proverb is not describing someone who lacks intelligence but someone who has refused the corrective process by which intelligence grows into wisdom. Two postures toward the self are on display — one that accepts being shaped, one that refuses it — and only the second earns this particular label.
QUESTION 7 OF 10
According to Proverbs 11:14, what is the source of safety for a people or nation?
PROVERBS 11:14
"For lack of guidance a nation falls, but victory is won through many advisers." The proverb is about structure, not individual brilliance. A single voice — however gifted — carries blind spots that a council can correct. 'Many advisers' is not a prescription for paralysis but a structural check on the idolatry of personal certainty. The warning applies equally to any leader, elder, or decision-maker who surrounds himself only with voices that agree with him.
QUESTION 8 OF 10 DETAIL
Proverbs 12:4 says that a wife who brings shame is like what to her husband?
PROVERBS 12:4
"A wife of noble character is her husband's crown, but a disgraceful wife is like decay in his bones." The contrast is structural: a crown sits on the exterior, conferring visible honor; decay operates unseen from within, slowly destroying what it inhabits. The Hebrew word for 'decay' (raqab — rottenness) describes a corruption that cannot be easily reversed. The proverb does not assess blame; it traces two trajectories. One marriage crowns; the other hollows out from the inside.
QUESTION 9 OF 10
Proverbs 10:22 says the blessing of the LORD makes rich and adds what alongside it?
PROVERBS 10:22
"The blessing of the LORD brings wealth, without painful toil for it." The proverb distinguishes not between wealth and poverty but between two pathways wealth may travel. Human striving can produce riches that carry hidden costs — anxiety, moral compromise, the exhaustion of relentless grasping. The LORD's blessing produces the same material outcome without that hidden freight. This is a theological claim about the nature of God's gifts: they arrive whole, not wrapped in the sorrow that self-secured wealth cannot shake.
QUESTION 10 OF 10 DETAIL
According to Proverbs 12:27, what does the slothful person specifically fail to do?
PROVERBS 12:27
"The lazy do not roast any game, but the diligent feed on the riches of the hunt." The proverb catches the sluggard at a revealing moment: he has actually obtained game — the hard part, one might think — but he will not follow through to cook it. His failure is not at the start but at the finish. This is a portrait of the sluggard who can begin but cannot complete, who gets close but lets the payoff slip. The diligent person carries the effort all the way through, and that is precisely where the meal lives.
