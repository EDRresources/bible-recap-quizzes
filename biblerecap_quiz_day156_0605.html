QUESTION 1 OF 10 DETAIL
At what specific time of day does the narrator of Proverbs 7 observe the foolish young man heading toward the adulteress's street corner?
PROVERBS 7:9
"In the twilight, in the evening, at the fall of night and darkness." The three-part description — twilight, evening, fall of night — slows the clock deliberately, making the reader feel the weight of the young man's choice. He does not stumble into danger; he walks toward it as day gives way to dark. The hour signals the eclipse of wisdom: the light by which the wise walk is fading, and he is choosing that moment to venture out.
QUESTION 2 OF 10
In Proverbs 7, what specific reason does the adulteress give to convince the young man that they can be together without discovery?
PROVERBS 7:19–20
"For my husband is not at home; he has gone on a long journey. He took his bag of money with him and will not be home till full moon." The detail of the full moon is calculated — she names a specific deadline, manufacturing a window of false safety. Her invitation is not impulsive; it is organized. The young man who accepts it is trusting the word of someone whose entire vocation is deception.
QUESTION 3 OF 10 DETAIL
The narrator of Proverbs 7 uses three animal images to describe the young man following the adulteress. Which set correctly identifies all three?
PROVERBS 7:22–23
"All at once he followed her like an ox going to the slaughter, like a deer stepping into a noose till an arrow pierces his liver, like a bird darting into a snare, little knowing it will cost him his life." Each image emphasizes oblivious passivity — the animals do not sense the danger until it is too late. The young man's tragedy is not violent rebellion but the quiet absence of the knowledge that would have saved him.
QUESTION 4 OF 10
In Proverbs 8, where does personified Wisdom position herself when she calls out to humanity?
PROVERBS 8:2–3
"On the heights along the way, where the paths meet, she takes her stand; beside the gates in front of the town, at the entrance of the portals she cries aloud." Wisdom is not hidden or hard to find. She stations herself at the most trafficked intersections of civic life — the gates where commerce is transacted and judgments are rendered. Every person who passes through the city cannot miss her. This makes the fool's rejection of her all the more culpable.
QUESTION 5 OF 10
In Proverbs 8, Wisdom makes a direct claim about the value of what she offers compared to precious metals. What does she say?
PROVERBS 8:19
"My fruit is better than fine gold; what I yield surpasses choice silver." The comparison is qualitative, not merely quantitative — Wisdom's yield exceeds not just ordinary gold but the finest grade. The harvest imagery ("fruit," "yield") suggests abundance and ongoing production, not a one-time gift. Wisdom does not compete with wealth by offering a spiritual consolation prize; she surpasses it on its own terms.
QUESTION 6 OF 10 DETAIL
In Proverbs 8:13, personified Wisdom describes what the fear of the LORD leads her to hate. Which answer correctly names the things she lists?
PROVERBS 8:13
"To fear the LORD is to hate evil; I hate pride and arrogance, evil behavior and perverse speech." What Wisdom hates flows directly from what she is — the fear of the LORD expressed as active revulsion toward its opposites. Pride and arrogance head the list, which is consistent with Proverbs' consistent diagnosis that pride is the root disposition from which other evil grows. The "perverted mouth" at the end closes the list by condemning the instrument through which wickedness most commonly operates.
QUESTION 7 OF 10
At the close of Proverbs 8, Wisdom states the consequence of finding her and the consequence of missing her. What does she say?
PROVERBS 8:35–36
"For those who find me find life and receive favor from the LORD. But those who fail to find me harm themselves; all who hate me love death." The logic is reversible and complete: to find Wisdom is to find life; to miss her is self-harm; to hate her is to love death. That last phrase is a withering diagnosis — those who reject Wisdom do not merely choose foolishness, they are actively choosing their own destruction, even if they cannot see it.
QUESTION 8 OF 10 DETAIL
Proverbs 9 opens with Wisdom having built her house. How many pillars has she hewn for it?
PROVERBS 9:1
"Wisdom has built her house; she has set up its seven pillars." Seven is the number of completion in Hebrew thought — Wisdom's dwelling is fully and perfectly constructed. The image also contrasts sharply with Folly later in the same chapter: Wisdom has built and furnished and prepared a feast; Folly has built nothing, only calling from a doorstep to invite passers-by into a house where the dead already dwell.
QUESTION 9 OF 10
In Proverbs 9, what is Folly's actual sales pitch to those who pass by her door, and what makes it spiritually dangerous?
PROVERBS 9:17–18
"Stolen water is sweet; food eaten in secret is pleasant. But little do they know that the dead are there, that her guests are deep in the realm of the dead." Folly does not describe what she offers as good or nourishing — she describes it as stolen and secret. The appeal is to transgression itself. The danger is not that she lies about the pleasure; the pleasure is real enough. The danger is what she withholds: verse 18 supplies it plainly, but those entering her door do not know it.
QUESTION 10 OF 10
Proverbs 9:7–9 contrasts what happens when you correct a mocker versus when you rebuke a wise person. What contrast does the text draw?
PROVERBS 9:7–8
"Whoever corrects a mocker invites insults; whoever rebukes the wicked incurs abuse. Do not rebuke mockers or they will hate you; rebuke the wise and they will love you." This is one of Proverbs' most searching diagnostic tests: the response to correction reveals whether wisdom is present. The mocker does not merely fail to improve — he turns on the corrector. The wise person does the opposite: they receive rebuke as a gift and respond with love. The same action (rebuke) produces opposite results in opposite hearts.
