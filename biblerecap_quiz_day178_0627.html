QUESTION 1
When God directed Elijah to go into hiding at the start of the drought, at what location did He command him to camp?
A The Kerith Ravine, east of the Jordan
B The wilderness of Beersheba near a broom tree
C A cave on Mount Horeb in the wilderness
D The town gate of Zarephath in Sidon
1 Kings 17:3
God told Elijah, "Leave here, turn eastward and hide in the Kerith Ravine, east of the Jordan." The brook provided water there until it dried up — at which point God redirected Elijah to Zarephath. Beersheba and the cave on Horeb come later in the story, in chapter 19.

QUESTION 2 DETAIL
According to 1 Kings 17:6, in what precise manner did the ravens supply Elijah with food at the Kerith Ravine?
A They brought him grain each morning and meat every third evening
B They brought him bread and meat in the morning and bread and meat in the evening
C They brought him flesh only, both morning and evening, without bread
D They brought him bread in the morning and water in the evening
1 Kings 17:6
The text is specific: "The ravens brought him bread and meat in the morning and bread and meat in the evening." Both bread and meat were provided twice daily. God's provision through unclean birds in a drought-stricken land demonstrates that He is not limited by natural means or ceremonial categories.

QUESTION 3
What was the widow of Zarephath doing when Elijah first encountered her?
A Drawing water from the city well for her son
B Praying at the city gate for God to send provision
C Gathering sticks at the town gate
D Weeping over her dying son's sickbed inside her home
1 Kings 17:10
When Elijah arrived at the town gate of Zarephath, "a widow was there gathering sticks." Elijah called to her and asked first for water, then for bread. Her response revealed she was preparing a final meal before expected starvation — a context that makes Elijah's request, and God's subsequent provision, all the more striking.

QUESTION 4 DETAIL
When Elijah asked the widow of Zarephath for bread, what did she say she had left?
A A small measure of barley and a cruse of olive oil
B Enough flour for one small loaf and no oil at all
C A bit of meal in a bowl and a few drops of oil in a flask
D A handful of flour in a jar and a little oil in a jug
1 Kings 17:12
The widow said, "I don't have any bread — only a handful of flour in a jar and a little oil in a jug." The miracle of God's ongoing provision was that these two minimal quantities never ran out until the drought ended — a picture of God sustaining life from what appears to be nothing.

QUESTION 5
When Ahab finally saw Elijah after years of drought, what accusation did he level at him?
A "Is that you, you troubler of Israel?"
B "You enemy of the king and the gods of Israel!"
C "You who have hidden yourself from us and left the people to die!"
D "You false prophet who serves a powerless God!"
1 Kings 18:17
Ahab greeted Elijah with "Is that you, you troubler of Israel?" Elijah's reply is theologically pointed: he turns the accusation back on Ahab, saying it is Ahab who has troubled Israel by abandoning God's commands and following the Baals. The true troubler was the unfaithful king, not the faithful prophet.

QUESTION 6 DETAIL
At Mount Carmel, Elijah challenged the false prophets summoned there. What was the precise breakdown of their numbers?
A 400 prophets of Baal and 400 prophets of Asherah
B 450 prophets of Baal and 400 prophets of Asherah
C 850 prophets of Baal, with no prophets of Asherah present
D 400 prophets of Baal and 450 prophets of Asherah
1 Kings 18:19
Elijah summoned "the 450 prophets of Baal and the 400 prophets of Asherah." The prophets of Asherah eat at Jezebel's table — a detail that underscores her personal role in promoting this idolatry. Elijah faced 850 false prophets alone, making God's vindication through fire all the more dramatic.

QUESTION 7
After fire from the LORD consumed Elijah's sacrifice on Mount Carmel, what became of the prophets of Baal?
A Elijah sent them into exile beyond the Jordan River
B Elijah brought them bound before King Ahab for royal judgment
C They scattered and fled toward Jezreel to seek Jezebel's protection
D Elijah had them seized and slaughtered at the Kishon River
1 Kings 18:40
Elijah commanded, "Seize the prophets of Baal. Don't let anyone get away!" They were seized and Elijah brought them down to the Kishon Valley and slaughtered them there. This is consonant with Deuteronomy's law on false prophets (Deut. 13:5) — and it is what triggers Jezebel's murderous threat in chapter 19.

QUESTION 8
What did Elijah ask God for when he collapsed under the broom tree in the wilderness?
A That God would send an angel to guide him to a place of safety
B That God would restore his strength and courage for the road ahead
C That God would take his life, saying he was no better than his ancestors
D That God would strike Jezebel dead before she could reach him
1 Kings 19:4
Elijah prayed, "I have had enough, LORD. Take my life; I am no better than my ancestors." The prophet who had just called fire from heaven now wanted to die. God's response is tender: He does not rebuke Elijah but sends an angel with food, twice, and simply says, "The journey is too much for you." God meets exhaustion with provision, not condemnation.

QUESTION 9
On Mount Horeb, after the great wind, the earthquake, and the fire had passed, how did God's presence finally come to Elijah?
A As a gentle whisper — a still, small voice
B As a pillar of cloud that settled at the mouth of the cave
C As a blinding light that caused Elijah to fall facedown on the ground
D As a dream that came to Elijah while he slept at the cave entrance
1 Kings 19:11–12
After the wind, earthquake, and fire — none of which contained God's voice — came "a gentle whisper" (KJV: "a still small voice"). When Elijah heard it, he wrapped his face in his cloak and stood at the cave entrance. The contrast is theologically rich: God is not found in the spectacular displays of power but in the quiet word — the same pattern by which He speaks through Scripture and proclamation today.

QUESTION 10
In 2 Chronicles 18, Jehoshaphat joined Ahab at Samaria, but before agreeing to march on Ramoth Gilead, what did Jehoshaphat insist on?
A That his own military commanders review and approve Ahab's battle plan
B That they first seek the word of the LORD
C That all four hundred prophets swear a binding oath before speaking
D That Ahab formalize their military alliance with a blood covenant
2 Chronicles 18:4
Jehoshaphat said to Ahab, "First seek the counsel of the LORD." This is Jehoshaphat's characteristic instinct (cf. 2 Chr. 17:4). Four hundred prophets then spoke with unified enthusiasm for war — but Jehoshaphat's further request for "a prophet of the LORD" led to Micaiah, whose true prophecy of defeat Ahab despised. The episode is a cautionary portrait of surrounding yourself only with those who tell you what you want to hear.
