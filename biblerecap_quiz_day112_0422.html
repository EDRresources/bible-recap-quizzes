QUESTION 1 OF 10 DETAIL
Psalm 6 is a prayer for healing and mercy. After opening with a plea not to be rebuked in anger, the psalmist immediately describes his distress. Which option accurately combines the two specific conditions he names in verses 2–3?
Psalm 6:2–3
"Be gracious to me, O LORD, for I am languishing; heal me, O LORD, for my bones are troubled. My soul also is greatly troubled" (Psalm 6:2–3). The Hebrew word for “troubled” (bâhal) suggests violent agitation — not mere sadness but inner upheaval. The psalmist’s distress spans body (bones) and spirit (soul), which is characteristic of the lament psalms’ holistic view of human suffering. Both dimensions together form a complete cry for healing that anticipates Jesus’s own deep soul-anguish in Gethsemane.
QUESTION 2 OF 10 DETAIL
Psalm 8:1–2 declares that God has established something specific “out of the mouth of babies and infants.” What has God established there, and for what stated purpose?
Psalm 8:1–2
"Out of the mouth of babies and infants, you have established strength because of your foes, to still the enemy and the avenger" (Psalm 8:2). The Hebrew can also be rendered “praise” — the LXX chose that word, and Jesus quotes it that way in Matthew 21:16 when children shout hosannas in the temple. The divine irony is profound: the God whose name is majestic above all the heavens uses the weakest voices to silence his mightiest enemies, a pattern that reaches its climax in the weakness of the cross defeating death (1 Cor. 1:25).
QUESTION 3 OF 10
Psalm 9 celebrates God’s justice against the wicked nations. The psalm uses a striking image of self-destruction to describe how the nations received judgment. Which option correctly identifies this image?
Psalm 9:15
"The nations have sunk in the pit that they made; in the net that they hid, their own foot has been caught" (Psalm 9:15). This retributive boomerang — the wicked undone by their own devices — runs throughout the Psalter and wisdom literature. It reaches its supreme expression in the cross: the powers that crucified Christ found that the cross was the instrument of their own defeat (Col. 2:15). The psalmist sees divine justice not merely as external punishment but as the wicked reaping the consequence of their own crafted evil.
QUESTION 4 OF 10 DETAIL
Psalm 10 contains a sustained portrait of the wicked person’s inner speech. Among the several self-congratulatory statements attributed to the wicked, which verse records his most explicit statement of false personal security regarding his own future?
Psalm 10:6
"He says in his heart, ‘I shall not be moved; throughout all generations I shall not meet adversity’" (Psalm 10:6). Psalm 10 does attribute several other godless thoughts to the wicked man (v. 4: “There is no God”; v. 11: “God has forgotten”; v. 13: “You will not call to account”), but verse 6 is his explicit statement of personal invulnerability. Remarkably, it inverts Psalm 16:8 — where the righteous man trusts in the LORD and declares “I shall not be moved.” The wicked man applies to himself what belongs only to one who trusts in God.
QUESTION 5 OF 10 DETAIL
After declaring that “there is none who does good, not even one,” Psalm 14 shifts from God’s heavenly diagnosis to a specific accusation against the evildoers concerning how they treat a particular group. What is the accusation?
Psalm 14:4
"Have they no knowledge, all the evildoers who eat up my people as they eat bread and do not call upon the LORD?" (Psalm 14:4). The image is chilling: devouring the vulnerable is as casual and routine as a meal. The two clauses are linked — failure to call upon the LORD directly produces the exploitation of people. Paul quotes Psalm 14:1–3 in Romans 3:10–12 as part of his sweeping indictment that all humanity stands guilty before God, making the gift of righteousness through Christ the only possible hope for Jew and Gentile alike.
QUESTION 6 OF 10
Psalm 16 expresses complete trust in the LORD as the psalmist’s sole good. The psalm also includes a specific negative vow regarding those who run after other gods. What does the psalmist refuse to do concerning those gods?
Psalm 16:4
"The sorrows of those who run after another god shall multiply; their drink offerings of blood I will not pour out or take their names on my lips" (Psalm 16:4). The refusal goes beyond behavior — even speaking a foreign deity’s name is off limits. This echoes Exodus 23:13 (“make no mention of the names of other gods”) and reveals how thoroughly exclusive the covenant relationship is. The sorrows of idolaters — multiplying — stand in stark contrast to the pleasant inheritance of the one who finds his portion in the LORD alone (v. 5–6).
QUESTION 7 OF 10 DETAIL
In Psalm 16:5–6, the psalmist uses the imagery of land allotment to express contentment with God as his portion. Which option most accurately captures what verse 6 specifically says about his inheritance?
Psalm 16:6
"The lines have fallen for me in pleasant places; indeed, I have a beautiful inheritance" (Psalm 16:6). The surveyor’s measuring lines used to mark tribal land portions in Canaan (cf. Joshua 17) become the psalmist’s metaphor for divine providence. Verse 5 identifies the LORD himself as his “chosen portion and cup,” and verse 6 declares the survey result: the land is pleasant, the inheritance beautiful. Peter quotes Psalm 16:8–11 at Pentecost (Acts 2:25–28) as a prophecy fulfilled in Christ’s resurrection — the ultimate inheritance that does not see corruption.
QUESTION 8 OF 10 DETAIL
Psalm 19:7–9 contains six parallel lines, each naming God’s word with a different title and pairing it with a quality or effect. Which of the following is NOT one of the six titles used to describe God’s word in this sequence?
Psalm 19:7–9
The six titles in Psalm 19:7–9 are: the law (tôrâ), the testimony (‘êdût), the precepts (piqqûdîm), the commandment (mişwâ), the fear (yir’â), and the rules / judgments (mišpâŧîm). “Wisdom” is never used as a title for God’s word in this sequence, though the psalm strongly implies it: v. 7 says the testimony “makes wise the simple.” Note also that “the fear of the LORD” appears here not as a description of human piety but as a name for the Torah itself — the revealed word that structures holy reverence.
QUESTION 9 OF 10
The closing prayer of Psalm 19 distinguishes between two categories of sin and makes specific petitions about each. What are the two categories, and what is the psalmist’s specific petition regarding the second, more dangerous one?
Psalm 19:12–13
"Who can discern his errors? Declare me innocent from hidden faults. Keep back your servant also from presumptuous sins; let them not have dominion over me!" (Psalm 19:12–13). Hidden faults (sêgî‘ôt) are errors committed without full awareness; presumptuous sins (zêdîm, “the insolent ones”) are deliberate, high-handed acts of rebellion. The petitions differ in kind: for hidden faults, the psalmist asks for acquittal; for presumptuous sins, he asks to be restrained — that they not “have dominion.” Paul echoes this exact word in Romans 6:14: “sin will have no dominion over you, since you are not under law but under grace.”
QUESTION 10 OF 10 DETAIL
Psalm 21 is a royal psalm of thanksgiving following victory. Verse 4 records something the king specifically asked of the LORD, and what God gave in response. What did the king ask for, and what precisely did God give?
Psalm 21:4
"He asked life of you; you gave it to him, length of days forever and ever" (Psalm 21:4). No historical king — not even David — received literal eternal life, which is why the church has read this as messianic. Peter’s Pentecost sermon declares that God did not abandon the Christ to death (Acts 2:31–32); the writer of Hebrews says Christ “holds his priesthood permanently, because he continues forever” (Heb 7:24). The crown of gold in verse 3 is a gift the LORD placed on the king’s head; the eternal life in verse 4 is what the king himself asked for — and only Christ has fully received that answer.
