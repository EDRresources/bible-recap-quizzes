QUESTION 1 OF 10
What cargo did Solomon's fleet of Tarshish ships bring back, arriving once every three years?
According to 1 Kings 10:22, Solomon's fleet of Tarshish ships came once every three years bearing gold, silver, ivory, apes, and peacocks (or baboons). This extraordinary menagerie illustrated the legendary reach of Solomon's commercial empire. Cedar wood and spices, while mentioned elsewhere in connection with Hiram's trade, are not part of this specific cargo list.
QUESTION 2 OF 10 DETAIL
For what two specific purposes did Solomon use the almug wood that arrived from Ophir via Hiram's ships?
1 Kings 10:11–12 records that Hiram's fleet brought almug wood from Ophir, and Solomon used it for two purposes: structural supports (pillars) for the temple and the royal palace, and for making musical instruments — lyres and harps for the singers. The text adds that no such almug wood had ever been seen before or since.
QUESTION 3 OF 10 DETAIL
According to 1 Kings 11:1, Solomon's foreign wives who led him astray came from many nations. Which of the following peoples is not listed among their origins?
1 Kings 11:1 lists Solomon's foreign wives as coming from Moab, Ammon, Edom, Sidon, and the Hittites. Philistines, though Israel's persistent enemy and geographically close, are conspicuously absent from this list. The nations named are those whose intermarriage with Israel the LORD had expressly forbidden, for they would turn Israel's hearts after other gods.
QUESTION 4 OF 10
In 1 Kings 11, what reason does God give for not tearing the kingdom away from Solomon during Solomon's own lifetime?
1 Kings 11:12 records God's word to Solomon: “Yet for the sake of David your father I will not do it in your days.” It is David, not Solomon's temple-building or any personal merit, that stays God's immediate judgment. Verse 13 reinforces the same reason: “for the sake of David my servant and for the sake of Jerusalem that I have chosen.” There is no record of Solomon repenting.
QUESTION 5 OF 10
What specific news prompted Hadad the Edomite to ask Pharaoh for permission to return to his homeland?
1 Kings 11:21 specifies that when Hadad “heard in Egypt that David slept with his fathers and that Joab the commander of the army was dead,” he asked Pharaoh to let him return. Pharaoh's puzzled response — “What have you lacked with me that you seek to go to your own country?” — underscores how completely Hadad had been absorbed into Egyptian court life since his childhood flight from Joab's slaughter.
QUESTION 6 OF 10
In 1 Kings 11:33, what specific charge does God level against Solomon when explaining the coming division of the kingdom?
1 Kings 11:33 gives God's precise indictment: “because they have forsaken me and worshiped Ashtoreth the goddess of the Sidonians, Chemosh the god of Moab, and Milcom the god of the Ammonites, and they have not walked in my ways, doing what is right in my sight and keeping my statutes and my rules, as David his father did.” The comparison to David's faithfulness makes Solomon's apostasy all the more stark.
QUESTION 7 OF 10 DETAIL
According to 1 Kings 11:7, which two gods did Solomon specifically build high places for on the mountain east of Jerusalem?
1 Kings 11:7 names the two deities whose high places Solomon built on the mountain east of Jerusalem: “a high place for Chemosh the abomination of Moab, and for Molech the abomination of the Ammonites.” Option B (Ashtoreth and Milcom) are mentioned in verse 5 as gods Solomon followed, but those specific high places are not placed on the eastern hill. Option D reverses the pair and uses “Milcom” instead of “Molech,” making it technically inaccurate to verse 7.
QUESTION 8 OF 10
What trading arrangement involving horses does 1 Kings 10:28–29 describe?
1 Kings 10:28–29 describes a sophisticated re-export trade: horses were imported from Egypt and Kue (Cilicia), then Solomon's traders exported them to the kings of the Hittites and the kings of Aram. A chariot cost 600 shekels of silver and a horse 150 shekels. This arrangement made Solomon a middleman in the ancient Near East's military supply chain — and violated Deuteronomy 17's warning against a king multiplying horses.
QUESTION 9 OF 10 DETAIL
What does 1 Kings 11:26 tell us about Jeroboam's parentage and family background?
1 Kings 11:26 introduces Jeroboam as “the son of Nebat, an Ephraimite of Zeredah, a servant of Solomon, whose mother's name was Zeruah, a widow.” His Ephraimite tribal identity is significant — it places him squarely within the northern tribes who would eventually follow him. The detail that his mother was a widow adds to the picture of Jeroboam as a self-made man who caught Solomon's eye through sheer industry.
QUESTION 10 OF 10
What was Solomon's annual income of gold, as recorded in both 1 Kings 10:14 and 2 Chronicles 9:13?
Both 1 Kings 10:14 and 2 Chronicles 9:13 agree precisely: Solomon's annual income of gold was 666 talents — a staggering figure. The other numbers are plausible distractors drawn from the same chapters: 420 talents is what Hiram's ships brought from Ophir (1 Kgs 10:11), 450 is the parallel figure in 2 Chronicles 9:13 for that shipment, and 120 talents was the Queen of Sheba's gift to Solomon.
