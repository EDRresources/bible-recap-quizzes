QUESTION 1 OF 10 DETAIL
Proverbs 4:7 says "The beginning of wisdom is this: Get wisdom." What does the father say to get even if "it cost all you have"?
PROVERBS 4:7
"The beginning of wisdom is this: Get wisdom. Though it cost all you have, get understanding." The verse is almost paradoxical — wisdom begins by getting wisdom — but the second imperative sharpens it: the single most important acquisition is understanding, worth every sacrifice. The parallelism between wisdom and understanding throughout Proverbs is tight, but here understanding is the costlier, final prize.
QUESTION 2 OF 10
In Proverbs 4, the father contrasts two ways of life. Which of the following best captures the contrast he draws between the path of the righteous and the way of the wicked?
PROVERBS 4:18–19
"The path of the righteous is like the morning sun, shining ever brighter till the full light of day. But the way of the wicked is like deep darkness; they do not know what makes them stumble." The imagery is precise: the righteous path is not merely lit but progressively brightening, while the wicked stumble in a darkness so total they cannot identify the cause of their own falling.
QUESTION 3 OF 10
In Proverbs 5, how does the father describe the lips and speech of the forbidden woman at first — and what does she ultimately become?
PROVERBS 5:3–4
"For the lips of the adulterous woman drip honey, and her speech is smoother than oil; but in the end she is bitter as gall, sharp as a double-edged sword." The contrast between initial sweetness and ultimate bitterness is the rhetorical spine of the warning. The word "end" ('acharit) is significant — it is the same word used for one's final destiny. What appears delightful in the moment leads to a deadly conclusion.
QUESTION 4 OF 10 DETAIL
Proverbs 5:9–10 warns of specific consequences for the man who gives himself to the adulteress. What does verse 10 say will happen to his wealth and labor?
PROVERBS 5:9–10
"Lest you give your best strength to others and your years to one who is cruel, lest strangers feast on your wealth and your toil enrich another man's house." The economic dimension of sexual sin is rarely preached but is explicit here: adultery bleeds resources. A man who abandons covenant faithfulness for the forbidden woman ends up laboring for the benefit of strangers, giving his best years and wealth to someone else's household.
QUESTION 5 OF 10
In Proverbs 6, the father tells the sluggard to consider the ant. What specific detail does the text give about the ant's governance that makes it a model of self-motivated diligence?
PROVERBS 6:6–8
"Go to the ant, you sluggard; consider its ways and be wise! It has no commander, no overseer or ruler, yet it stores its provisions in summer and gathers its food at harvest." The key detail is the absence of external authority — no commander, no overseer, no ruler. The ant's virtue is internal and self-directed. The sluggard, who needs to be told to get up, is shamed by a creature that requires no instruction at all.
QUESTION 6 OF 10
Proverbs 6:16–19 lists seven things the LORD hates. Which of the following does NOT appear in that list?
PROVERBS 6:16–19
The seven things listed are: haughty eyes, a lying tongue, hands that shed innocent blood, a heart that devises wicked schemes, feet that are quick to rush into evil, a false witness who pours out lies, and a person who stirs up conflict in the community. "A proud heart that exalts itself" is not in the list — though haughty eyes (pride expressed through the eyes) is. The heart entry is specifically a heart that devises wicked schemes, not pride per se.
QUESTION 7 OF 10 DETAIL
In Proverbs 6, the "troublemaker and villain" is said to communicate corruption through specific physical gestures rather than open speech. Which set of gestures does the text list?
PROVERBS 6:12–13
"A troublemaker and a villain, who goes about with a corrupt mouth, who winks maliciously with his eye, signals with his feet and motions with his fingers." The three gestures — eye, feet, fingers — suggest a system of coded, non-verbal communication used to coordinate deceit. He does not speak openly; he corrupts through signals. The corrupt mouth goes with a body that conspires silently.
QUESTION 8 OF 10
How does Proverbs 6 compare a thief who steals out of hunger to a man who commits adultery?
PROVERBS 6:30–32
"People do not despise a thief if he steals to satisfy his hunger when he is starving. Yet if he is caught, he must pay sevenfold... But a man who commits adultery has no sense; whoever does so destroys himself." The comparison is not meant to excuse theft but to heighten the folly of adultery. Theft at least has a comprehensible motive — survival. Adultery is pure self-destruction with no mitigating logic; it is the act of a man who has abandoned all wisdom.
QUESTION 9 OF 10
Proverbs 4:23 instructs the son: "Above all else, guard your heart." What reason does the text give for this command?
PROVERBS 4:23
"Above all else, guard your heart, for everything you do flows from it." The Hebrew word translated "flows" (totsa'ot) literally means "outgoings" or "issues" — the sources of life stream outward from the heart. All conduct, speech, and character originates there. Choices A and C, while true elsewhere in Scripture (Jeremiah 17:9; Romans 2:16), are not the reason Proverbs 4 gives. The text's logic is anthropological, not judicial: the heart is the fountain of all action.
QUESTION 10 OF 10
According to Proverbs 6:34–35, what does the jealous husband of the adulteress do when his honor is violated?
PROVERBS 6:34–35
"For jealousy arouses a husband's fury, and he will show no mercy when he takes revenge. He will not accept any compensation; he will refuse a bribe, however great it is." The warning is deliberately extreme: unlike theft, which can be settled by repayment, adultery sets in motion a jealousy that money cannot extinguish. No amount of compensation bridges the violation. This is the closing argument for why the man who commits adultery "destroys himself" (v. 32) — the consequences are beyond negotiation.
