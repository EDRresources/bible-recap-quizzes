QUESTION 1 OF 10 DETAIL
Psalm 121 promises that two natural forces will not harm the LORD's protected traveler. Which option correctly pairs each threat with the period it corresponds to?
Psalm 121:6
"The sun shall not strike you by day, nor the moon by night" (Psalm 121:6). Ancient peoples feared both the scorching power of the sun and the mysterious influence attributed to the moon at night (hence the word “lunacy”). Together they form a merism for all threats across the full span of time — the psalm's point is that the LORD's protective care is all-encompassing, day and night without exception.
QUESTION 2 OF 10 DETAIL
In Psalm 123, the community's upward gaze toward God is compared to a servant's gaze toward a master. What specific part of the master is the servant's gaze directed toward in this comparison?
Psalm 123:2
"As the eyes of servants look to the hand of their master, as the eyes of a maidservant to the hand of her mistress, so our eyes look to the LORD our God, until he has mercy upon us" (Psalm 123:2). In the ancient household, servants watched the master's hand for gestured signals — a subtle movement that could direct, command, or grant. The image conveys utter attentiveness and complete dependence, not passive helplessness but alert, expectant trust directed toward the one who can act.
QUESTION 3 OF 10
Psalm 124 uses three successive images of violent water to describe the fate Israel narrowly escaped. In what order does the psalmist present these images?
Psalm 124:4–5
"Then the flood would have swept us away, the torrent would have gone over us; then over us would have gone the raging waters" (Psalm 124:4–5). Each image escalates the threat — from an overwhelming surge to a rushing current to furious, billowing waters. The repeated "then" (Hebrew ‘azai) marks a sequential, mounting tide of danger from which only the LORD's intervention could save. The water metaphor recalls the chaos of the sea in creation and the Exodus crossing.
QUESTION 4 OF 10 DETAIL
After the water metaphors, Psalm 124 shifts to a completely different image for Israel's escape. What is the image, and what has happened to the instrument of captivity?
Psalm 124:7
"We have escaped like a bird from the snare of the fowler; the snare is broken, and we have escaped!" (Psalm 124:7). The shift from drowning water to a hidden trap underscores the variety of threats the LORD has defeated. The doubled "we have escaped" — beginning and ending the verse — conveys breathless, jubilant relief. Crucially, it is not that the bird broke free by its own strength: the snare was broken by another. Israel's deliverance is entirely from outside itself.
QUESTION 5 OF 10
Psalm 125 uses two geographical features of the land to make theological claims. Which option correctly identifies both comparisons the psalmist makes?
Psalm 125:1–2
"Those who trust in the LORD are like Mount Zion, which cannot be moved, but abides forever. As the mountains surround Jerusalem, so the LORD surrounds his people, from this time forth and forevermore" (Psalm 125:1–2). The trusted one (Israel) is compared to the immovable mountain itself; the protector (the LORD) is the ring of mountains that makes Jerusalem naturally defensible. Remarkably, God does not merely inhabit one mountain — he is the entire surrounding range.
QUESTION 6 OF 10 DETAIL
Psalm 125 promises that "the scepter of wickedness shall not rest on the land allotted to the righteous" and gives a specific reason for this assurance. What reason does the psalm provide?
Psalm 125:3
"For the scepter of wickedness shall not rest on the land allotted to the righteous, lest the righteous stretch out their hands to do wrong" (Psalm 125:3). This is a striking pastoral reason: prolonged oppression could tempt even the righteous to compromise, collaborate, or accommodate evil. God limits the duration of affliction not only to vindicate the innocent but to protect the faithful from the spiritual danger of gradual capitulation. The promise is pastoral before it is political.
QUESTION 7 OF 10 DETAIL
Psalm 128 describes the domestic blessings of the man who fears the LORD using two agricultural images. Which option correctly identifies both images?
Psalm 128:3
"Your wife will be like a fruitful vine within your house; your children will be like olive shoots around your table" (Psalm 128:3). Both images are agricultural: the vine that produces abundant fruit from within the household, and the olive tree that sends up productive shoots clustered at the base of the parent. The psalm frames domestic prosperity as a gift that flows organically from the fear of the LORD — not from personal ambition or achievement, but from a rightly ordered relationship with God.
QUESTION 8 OF 10 DETAIL
Psalm 129 personifies Israel as a speaker recounting long suffering. The psalm uses a vivid agricultural metaphor to describe the oppression Israel has endured. What is that metaphor?
Psalm 129:3
"The plowers plowed upon my back; they made long their furrows" (Psalm 129:3). The image is viscerally violent — Israel's back treated as a field to be worked over, furrowed deep by the oppressor's plow. Yet the very next verse reverses the power dynamic entirely: "The LORD is righteous; he has cut the cords of the wicked" (v.4). The instrument of oppression is turned back on those who wielded it. Israel survives not by strength but because God severs the cords that bind the plow to the plower.
QUESTION 9 OF 10
Psalm 129 closes with a wish that those who hate Zion would be like grass growing on housetops. The psalmist makes two specific points about the uselessness of this grass. Which option correctly captures both?
Psalm 129:6–7
"Let them be like the grass on the housetops, which withers before it grows up, with which the reaper does not fill his hand nor the binder of sheaves his arms" (Psalm 129:6–7). Housetop grass germinates in the thin soil of flat mud roofs but scorches before it can mature. The psalm then specifies the agricultural consequence: no reaper stoops for a handful, no binder bundles it into sheaves. The point is not destruction by force — it is utter futility. Those who oppose Zion produce nothing that can be counted, gathered, or kept.
QUESTION 10 OF 10
Psalm 130:4 makes a theologically counterintuitive statement about the purpose of divine forgiveness. Which option correctly states what the verse says and why it is surprising?
Psalm 130:4
"But with you there is forgiveness, that you may be feared" (Psalm 130:4). The logic runs against instinct: one might expect that forgiveness would diminish the fear of God, since punishment is withheld. The psalmist insists the opposite — it is the experience of undeserved, freely given forgiveness that produces the deepest, most genuine reverence. Luther called this verse central to understanding true repentance, noting that it is God's grace, not his wrath alone, that draws the sinner into holy awe. Paul echoes the same logic: "the kindness of God leads you to repentance" (Romans 2:4), and the great NT declaration of forgiveness in Romans 3 culminates not in casual ease but in doxology and transformed life.
