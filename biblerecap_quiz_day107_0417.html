QUESTION 1 OF 10 DETAIL

The text describes Nabal as a very wealthy man. How many sheep and how many goats did he own?

1 Samuel 25:2
The text records that Nabal had three thousand sheep and a thousand goats. His enormous flocks were being sheared at the very moment David made his request, making Nabal's stingy refusal all the more striking. He had abundance to spare and gave nothing.
QUESTION 2 OF 10

When David's men brought his request to Nabal, Nabal refused and insulted David. What specific accusation did Nabal make against David in his refusal?

1 Samuel 25:10–11
Nabal sneered, "Who is David?... There are many servants nowadays who are breaking away from their masters." He dismissed David as nothing more than a runaway slave -- a calculated insult against the man God had already anointed as Israel's future king.
QUESTION 3 OF 10 DETAIL

How many young men did David send to deliver his request to Nabal?

1 Samuel 25:5
David sent ten young men, instructing them to greet Nabal in his name and make the request respectfully. The delegation's size was diplomatically deliberate -- enough to represent David's authority, but not so large as to appear threatening.
QUESTION 4 OF 10

In her appeal to David, Abigail used a vivid image to describe how God would deal with David's enemies. What was that image?

1 Samuel 25:29
Abigail told David that God would "sling out the lives of your enemies as from the hollow of a sling." The image carries double weight: the sling was David's own famous weapon against Goliath, and now Abigail uses it to urge David to trust God for vengeance rather than seizing it himself.
QUESTION 5 OF 10

When Abigail returned home after secretly meeting David, she found Nabal in a particular condition. What was it, and what did she do as a result?

1 Samuel 25:36
Nabal was holding a great feast "like the feast of a king," and "he was very drunk." So Abigail "told him nothing at all until the morning light." Her restraint showed wisdom -- a drunk Nabal could not meaningfully receive the news. She waited for sobriety before delivering the sobering truth.
QUESTION 6 OF 10

The morning after his feast, when Abigail told Nabal what she had done to avert David's attack, what happened to Nabal physically?

1 Samuel 25:37–38
Upon hearing the news, "his heart died within him, and he became as a stone." About ten days later, the LORD struck Nabal and he died. The text presents his physical collapse as divine judgment -- God avenged David without David having to lift a hand, exactly as Abigail had promised.
QUESTION 7 OF 10

When David and Abishai crept into Saul's camp at night, no one detected them. What reason does the text give for why the entire camp slept so soundly?

1 Samuel 26:12
The text states plainly: "no man saw it or knew it, nor did any awake, for they were all asleep, because a deep sleep from the LORD had fallen upon them." God himself subdued three thousand armed soldiers, making David's deliberate restraint toward Saul all the more striking.
QUESTION 8 OF 10 DETAIL

David took two specific items from beside the sleeping Saul as proof that he had been close enough to kill the king. What were those two items?

1 Samuel 26:11–12
David took "the spear and the jar of water from Saul's head" -- both objects at the king's most exposed position while he slept. When David displayed them from a safe hilltop, they served as undeniable evidence of how close he had come, and of his conscious choice not to strike.
QUESTION 9 OF 10

After David called out from a distance and exposed that he had spared Saul's life again, what was Saul's response?

1 Samuel 26:21, 25
Saul confessed, "I have sinned. Return, my son David, for I will no more do you harm... I have acted foolishly, and have made a great mistake." He then blessed David: "You will do great things and surely triumph." Yet David did not return -- he had heard such words from Saul before and knew better than to trust them.
QUESTION 10 OF 10 DETAIL

After David fled to Philistine territory and Achish gave him Ziklag as a base, how long did the text say David lived in the land of the Philistines?

1 Samuel 27:7
The text states precisely: "the number of days that David lived in the country of the Philistines was a year and four months." During this time David raided Israel's enemies while deceiving Achish into thinking he was raiding Judah -- leaving no survivors who could contradict his story. It was a morally complicated season in David's life.
