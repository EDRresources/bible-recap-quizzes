QUESTION 1 OF 10 DETAIL

In Psalm 17, David appeals to God to vindicate him. What specific action does David say God performed during the night as evidence of his integrity?

Psalm 17:3
David declares: "You have tried my heart, you have visited me by night, you have tested me, and you will find nothing; I have purposed that my mouth will not transgress." The nighttime visit is divine examination -- God scrutinizes David's inmost heart and comes away finding no wickedness. This is David's boldest claim: he is innocent of the charges his enemies press against him.
QUESTION 2 OF 10

In Psalm 17, David asks God to protect him using a tender and intimate image. What does David ask God to keep him as, and what does he ask God to hide him under?

Psalm 17:8
David pleads: "Keep me as the apple of your eye; hide me in the shadow of your wings." "Apple of the eye" refers to the pupil -- the most sensitive and carefully protected part of the eye. Together with the sheltering image of wings (as a mother bird protects her young), this verse expresses both David's vulnerability and his confidence in God's fierce, tender protection.
QUESTION 3 OF 10 DETAIL

Psalm 17 concludes with a striking contrast between the wicked and the righteous regarding what they receive. What does David say about the wicked in this contrast, and what is his own hope?

Psalm 17:14–15
David writes of wicked men "whose portion is in this life" -- their bellies are filled with God's treasure, their children have plenty, yet that earthly reward is all they get. By contrast: "As for me, I shall behold your face in righteousness; when I awake, I shall be satisfied with your likeness." David's hope transcends this life. Many see in verse 15 an early glimpse of resurrection hope -- waking to see God's face in full.
QUESTION 4 OF 10

In Psalm 35, David asks God to fight on his behalf using military imagery. Which specific weapons does David ask God to take up when rising to his defense?

Psalm 35:2–3
David calls out: "Take hold of shield and buckler and rise for my help! Draw the spear and javelin against my pursuers! Say to my soul, 'I am your salvation!'" The imagery is of God suiting up as a warrior champion. David doesn't just ask for divine aid -- he asks God to personally arm himself and charge into battle. The request that God speak assurance -- "I am your salvation" -- reveals that David needs both rescue and the word that assures him rescue is coming.
QUESTION 5 OF 10 DETAIL

Psalm 35 reveals something striking about how David had previously behaved toward these same enemies who now attack him. What did David do when they were sick?

Psalm 35:13–14
David testifies: "But I, when they were sick -- I wore sackcloth; I afflicted myself with fasting; I prayed with head bowed on my chest. I went about as though I grieved for my friend or my brother; I bowed down heavily, as one who laments his mother." The depth of David's former compassion -- mourning like a bereaved family member -- makes the ingratitude of these enemies all the more bitter and all the more an injustice requiring God's intervention.
QUESTION 6 OF 10

Psalm 35 contains a verse quoted by Jesus in the New Testament to describe his own experience. Which phrase from Psalm 35 does Jesus apply to himself, and where in the Gospels does he quote it?

Psalm 35:19; John 15:25
David writes in Psalm 35:19: "Let not those rejoice over me who are wrongfully my foes, and let not those wink the eye who hate me without cause." Jesus quotes this exact phrase in John 15:25 -- "They hated me without a cause" -- to explain the world's hostility toward him and his disciples. David's unjust suffering prefigures the perfect innocence of the one his enemies condemned. What was true of David the type is supremely true of Christ the antitype.
QUESTION 7 OF 10 DETAIL

The superscription of Psalm 54 provides a specific historical occasion for the psalm. What event prompted David to write it?

Psalm 54 superscription; 1 Samuel 23:19
The superscription reads: "A Maskil of David, when the Ziphites went and told Saul, 'Is not David hiding among us?'" The Ziphites, fellow Judahites, betrayed the location of their kinsman David to Saul -- not once but twice (see also 1 Sam 26). This treachery from within his own tribe made the wound sharper. Psalm 54 is David's raw, urgent cry for God to save him from the consequences of this betrayal.
QUESTION 8 OF 10

In Psalm 54, David quickly moves from crisis to confidence. What does he identify as the turning point -- the single theological affirmation that anchors his hope amid the threat?

Psalm 54:4
"Behold, God is my helper; the Lord is the upholder of my life." After only two verses of lament, David pivots on this single "Behold" -- an invitation to redirect attention from the threat to the Savior. "Upholder of my life" (ESV) renders a Hebrew word meaning the one who sustains or props up -- God is the structural support that keeps David from collapse. This one affirmation is enough to shift the entire psalm's tone from plea to praise.
QUESTION 9 OF 10 DETAIL

Psalm 63 opens with David expressing an intense, physical longing for God. In addition to his soul thirsting for God, what physical sensation does David use to describe his longing, and where is he when he writes this?

Psalm 63:1 (superscription)
The superscription places David "in the wilderness of Judah." He opens: "O God, you are my God; earnestly I seek you; my soul thirsts for you; my flesh faints for you, as in a dry and weary land where there is no water." The physical setting -- parched desert -- becomes the metaphor for spiritual longing. His body knows what thirst feels like; his soul aches for God with exactly that same urgency. The outer landscape mirrors the inner cry.
QUESTION 10 OF 10

In Psalm 63, David makes a remarkable declaration about the relative value of God's steadfast love compared to something else. What does he say God's steadfast love is better than, and what is his response to this reality?

Psalm 63:3
"Because your steadfast love is better than life, my lips will praise you." This is one of the most radical declarations in all of Scripture. Life itself -- the most fundamental good, the thing every creature clings to -- is declared inferior to God's covenant love (Hebrew: *hesed*). David says: even if staying alive cost me God's love, it would not be worth the price. The response is praise -- not because life is being preserved, but because *hesed* is present. This verse anchors the entire psalm's confidence even in the wilderness.
