Question 1 of 10
Proverbs 25 opens with a note identifying who transmitted these proverbs of Solomon to their present written form. Who was responsible?
Proverbs 25:1
"These are more proverbs of Solomon, compiled by the men of Hezekiah king of Judah." Hezekiah reigned centuries after Solomon, making this an extraordinary act of literary and theological preservation. His scribal reform sits alongside his spiritual revival — a reminder that guarding the written word of wisdom is itself an act of faithfulness to God.
Question 2 of 10
According to Proverbs 25:13, what is a faithful messenger like to those who send him? DETAIL
Proverbs 25:13
"Like the cold of snow in harvest time is a trustworthy messenger to the one who sends him; he refreshes the spirit of his master." "Cold water to a thirsty soul" is the comparison in verse 25 — but there it describes good news from a distant land, not a faithful messenger. The snow-in-harvest image vividly captures something precious and unexpected: snow is a rare relief in the Judean summer, making it a perfect emblem for the rare gift of total reliability.
Question 3 of 10
What does Proverbs 25 warn about when it comes to finding honey?
Proverbs 25:16
"If you find honey, eat just enough — too much of it, and you will vomit." The same principle is extended in verse 17 to visits with a neighbor: go too often and he will begin to hate you. Proverbs consistently argues that excess corrupts what is genuinely good — even legitimate blessings become harmful when they override self-restraint.
Question 4 of 10
What does Proverbs 25:21–22 instruct you to do if your enemy is hungry or thirsty, and what result does it promise?
Proverbs 25:21–22
"If your enemy is hungry, give him food to eat; if he is thirsty, give him water to drink. In doing this, you will heap burning coals on his head, and the LORD will reward you." Paul quotes this directly in Romans 12:20, embedding it in his teaching on overcoming evil with good. The "burning coals" imagery likely points to the searing shame or conviction that unexpected kindness produces — not a vindictive punishment but a disarming grace.
Question 5 of 10
Proverbs 26:4–5 contains an apparent contradiction, offering opposite advice about answering a fool. What specific reason does verse 4 give for NOT answering a fool?
Proverbs 26:4–5
"Do not answer a fool according to his folly, or you yourself will be just like him. Answer a fool according to his folly, or he will be wise in his own eyes." The two reasons are precisely matched to two distinct dangers. Verse 4's danger is self-corruption — descend to the fool's level and you absorb his foolishness. Verse 5's danger is leaving his pride unchecked — silence can confirm him in his delusion. Wisdom requires reading the situation to know which danger is greater.
Question 6 of 10
What image does Proverbs 26:11 use for the fool who repeats his folly? (This verse is quoted in the New Testament.)
Proverbs 26:11; 2 Peter 2:22
"As a dog returns to its vomit, so fools repeat their folly." Peter cites this proverb in 2 Peter 2:22 to describe false teachers who, after seeming to escape the corruption of the world, turn back to it. The pig in the mud appears in the same verse in 2 Peter — but that image is not from Proverbs 26. Proverbs uses the dog, not the pig, making this a test of close reading across both Testaments.
Question 7 of 10
Proverbs 26 describes the sluggard in several vivid ways. Which of the following is NOT one of the descriptions given in chapter 26? DETAIL
Proverbs 26:13–16; cf. Proverbs 10:5
The sleeping-through-harvest image appears in Proverbs 10:5 — "He who sleeps during harvest is a disgraceful son" — but not in chapter 26. Chapter 26's sluggard sequence (vv.13–16) gives us: the lion excuse (v.13), turning on his bed like a door (v.14), the hand buried in the dish (v.15), and the claim of being wiser than seven men who answer sensibly (v.16). The harvest image is a common association but comes from a different chapter.
Question 8 of 10
According to Proverbs 26:12, what type of person has even less hope than a fool?
Proverbs 26:12
"Do you see a person wise in their own eyes? There is more hope for a fool than for them." All three wrong answers describe behaviors that Proverbs 26 does condemn — slander (v.20), flattery (v.28), and hiring a fool (v.10). But the person without self-awareness is in the most hopeless position of all: a fool at least might recognize his need for wisdom, but the self-satisfied person has closed himself off from the very possibility of correction.
Question 9 of 10
What image does Proverbs 25:28 use for a person who lacks self-control?
Proverbs 25:28
"Like a city whose walls are broken through is a person who lacks self-control." In the ancient world, a city without walls was not merely inconvenient — it was defenseless, exposed to every invader. The image of a ruined city wall applies that same total vulnerability to the inner life: without self-mastery, a person has no defense against the passions, temptations, and influences that would destroy him. The other images are vivid but not what Proverbs uses here.
Question 10 of 10
How does Proverbs 26:18–19 describe the person who deceives his neighbor and then claims "I was only joking"? DETAIL
Proverbs 26:18–19
"Like a maniac shooting flaming arrows or deadly arrows is one who deceives their neighbor and says, 'I was only joking!'" The image is deliberately extreme: a madman indiscriminately hurling lethal, burning projectiles. Proverbs refuses to excuse harmful deception under the cover of humor — the damage caused by cruel "jokes" is real, and the joking defense does not diminish the injury. The pit-digger image appears in verse 27 but is used for a different point (retribution, not excuse-making).
