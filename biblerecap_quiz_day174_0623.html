QUESTION 1 OF 10
What did the assembly of Israel ask Rehoboam when they gathered at Shechem?
1 Kings 12:4 — “Your father made our yoke heavy. Now therefore lighten the hard service of your father and his heavy yoke on us, and we will serve you.” The people were not rebelling; they were offering conditional loyalty. Rehoboam had a genuine opportunity to rule with wisdom, and his choice to reject sound counsel set the course of Israel's history.
QUESTION 2 OF 10
Rehoboam rejected the counsel of the elders who had served his father. Whose advice did he follow instead?
1 Kings 12:8–10 — Rehoboam rejected the elders and consulted the young men who had grown up with him. Their advice was to tell the people his little finger was thicker than his father's waist. The elders understood statecraft; the young men understood flattery. The contrast between wise counsel and youthful pride could hardly be drawn more sharply.
QUESTION 3 OF 10 DETAIL
Rehoboam warned the people that his father had disciplined them with whips. What did he threaten to use instead?
1 Kings 12:14 — “My father disciplined you with whips, but I will discipline you with scorpions.” Note that verse 15 immediately adds: “So the king did not listen to the people, for it was a turn of affairs brought about by the LORD that he might fulfill his word” — the word he had spoken to Jeroboam through Ahijah. Rehoboam's pride was real, and God's sovereignty was no less real.
QUESTION 4 OF 10
When Rehoboam mustered 180,000 warriors to fight Jeroboam and reclaim the northern tribes, God sent the prophet Shemaiah to stop him. What reason did God give for forbidding the war?
1 Kings 12:24 — “Thus says the LORD, You shall not go up or fight against your relatives the people of Israel. Every man return to his home, for this thing is from me.” The division of the kingdom was God's sovereign act — the consequence of Solomon's unfaithfulness. Even Rehoboam's folly and Jeroboam's ambition served the divine purpose.
QUESTION 5 OF 10 DETAIL
Jeroboam set up two golden calves so his people would not travel to Jerusalem to worship. In which two cities did he place them?
1 Kings 12:28–29 — Jeroboam placed one calf in Bethel (the southern end of his kingdom, near Judah) and one in Dan (the far north). His proclamation — “Here are your gods, O Israel, who brought you up out of the land of Egypt” — deliberately echoed Aaron's sin at Sinai word for word (Exodus 32:4). This invented religion became the defining iniquity of the northern kingdom for every generation that followed.
QUESTION 6 OF 10
A man of God came from Judah and prophesied against Jeroboam's altar at Bethel. What sign did he give that the altar would one day be desecrated?
1 Kings 13:3, 5 — “This is the sign that the LORD has spoken: Behold, the altar shall be torn down, and the ashes that are on it shall be poured out.” When Jeroboam stretched out his hand to seize the man of God, his hand withered, and the altar was split and the ashes poured out — the sign fulfilled on the spot. The prophecy of Josiah by name (v.2) was fulfilled some 300 years later in 2 Kings 23.
QUESTION 7 OF 10 DETAIL
God commanded the man of God not to eat, drink, or return by the way he came. How did the old prophet of Bethel deceive him into disobeying?
1 Kings 13:18 — “He said to him, 'I also am a prophet as you are, and an angel spoke to me by the word of the LORD, saying, “Bring him back with you into your house that he may eat bread and drink water.”' But he lied to him.” The text does not soften it: he lied. This is a sobering warning that a clear word already given by God is not to be set aside because of a newer “revelation,” even from a fellow prophet.
QUESTION 8 OF 10
What judgment did God announce against the man of God for disobeying his command by eating in Bethel?
1 Kings 13:21–22 — The word came while they were still at the table: “your body shall not come to the tomb of your fathers.” The judgment was delivered through the very prophet who had deceived him. The man of God had faithfully delivered God's word and performed signs — and then fell through trusting a lying voice over a clear command. The irony is sharp and the warning lasting.
QUESTION 9 OF 10
What made the scene on the road after the man of God was struck down especially remarkable?
1 Kings 13:24–28 — “The lion had not eaten the body or mauled the donkey.” A predator that kills but does not consume was unmistakably supernatural. This restraint confirmed that the man's death was divine judgment rather than ordinary misfortune, and it moved the old prophet to honor the body and believe in earnest that the prophecy against Bethel would surely come to pass.
QUESTION 10 OF 10
According to Ahijah's prophecy in 1 Kings 14, why would Jeroboam's sick son Abijah be the only member of the household to receive a proper burial and mourning?
1 Kings 14:13 — “All Israel shall mourn for him and bury him, for he alone of Jeroboam's family shall come to the grave, because in him there is found something pleasing to the LORD, the God of Israel, in the house of Jeroboam.” The text does not specify what was pleasing — only that God saw it. The rest of Jeroboam's line would be swept away unburied. Even in the midst of wholesale judgment on a corrupt dynasty, God's grace marks out those who belong to him.
