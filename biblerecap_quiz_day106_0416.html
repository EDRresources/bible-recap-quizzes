QUESTION 1 OF 10 DETAIL

In Psalm 56, what does the psalmist ask God to do with his tears?

Psalm 56:8
The psalmist prays, "Record my misery; list my tears on your scroll — are they not in your record?" This intimate image presents God as one who keeps careful account of every sorrow his people experience.
QUESTION 2 OF 10

According to Psalm 56:13, from what two things does the psalmist say God delivered him?

Psalm 56:13
The psalm closes: "For you have delivered me from death and my feet from stumbling, that I may walk before God in the light of life." Both death and stumbling represent the twin dangers threatening the psalmist.
QUESTION 3 OF 10 DETAIL

What specific refrain appears twice in Psalm 56 — once near the beginning and once near the end?

Psalm 56:4, 11
The refrain "In God I trust and am not afraid. What can man do to me?" appears in verse 4 and again in verse 11, bracketing the psalm's central confession of trust in the midst of mortal danger.
QUESTION 4 OF 10 DETAIL

In Psalm 120, what two specific places does the psalmist say he dwells, lamenting his life among enemies?

Psalm 120:5
"Woe to me that I dwell in Meshech, that I live among the tents of Kedar!" These distant, hostile peoples — one near the Black Sea, one in the Arabian desert — represent the psalmist's sense of living far from peace and surrounded by those who love war.
QUESTION 5 OF 10

According to Psalm 120:4, with what will the LORD punish the deceitful tongue?

Psalm 120:4
"He will punish you with a warrior's sharp arrows, with burning coals of the broom bush." The broom bush was known for producing especially hot, long-lasting coals — an image of thorough, consuming judgment for the lying tongue.
QUESTION 6 OF 10 DETAIL

Psalm 140:9 describes a specific judgment on those who surround the psalmist. What does it say should engulf them?

Psalm 140:9
"Those who surround me proudly rear their heads; may the mischief of their lips engulf them." It is not fire or flood but the very evil their own lips have plotted that becomes their undoing — a fitting poetic justice.
QUESTION 7 OF 10

In Psalm 141:2, the psalmist compares his prayer and the lifting of his hands to two things. What are they?

Psalm 141:2
"May my prayer be set before you like incense; may the lifting up of my hands be like the evening sacrifice." This verse became influential in early Christian worship, connecting prayer with the temple's daily rhythm of incense and sacrifice.
QUESTION 8 OF 10 DETAIL

In Psalm 141:5, the psalmist welcomes something that he compares to "oil on my head" — saying his head "will not refuse it." What is this welcome thing?

Psalm 141:5
"Let a righteous man strike me — that is a kindness; let him rebuke me — that is oil on my head. My head will not refuse it." The psalmist sees correction from the godly as a gift — more valuable than the flattery of evildoers — and compares it to the honor of being anointed.
QUESTION 9 OF 10 DETAIL

In Psalm 142, when the psalmist looks to his right for help, what does he find?

Psalm 142:4
"Look and see, there is no one at my right hand; no one is concerned for me. I have no refuge; no one cares for my life." In the ancient world, a defender stood at one's right hand; the psalmist finds that position empty — making his cry to God all the more urgent.
QUESTION 10 OF 10

At the end of Psalm 142, the psalmist asks God to set him free from his "prison." What does he say will happen immediately after this freedom comes?

Psalm 142:7
"Set me free from my prison, that I may praise your name. Then the righteous will gather about me because of your bounty to me." The psalmist envisions his deliverance as both a personal act of worship and a communal event — the righteous community drawn together by God's generous grace.
