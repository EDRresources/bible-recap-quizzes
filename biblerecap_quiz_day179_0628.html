QUESTION 1
Ahab initially agreed to Ben-hadad's demands for silver, gold, wives, and children. What escalated the dispute and caused Ahab to refuse?
A Ben-hadad demanded Ahab's military commanders surrender their weapons to Aramean officers
B Ben-hadad raised the tribute demand to double what Ahab had agreed to pay
C Ben-hadad's servants declared they would search Ahab's palace and his officials' houses and take whatever they pleased
D Ben-hadad demanded Ahab send hostages to Damascus as surety against future rebellion
1 Kings 20:5–6
Ben-hadad sent a second message: his servants would come the next day and search Ahab's palace and his officials' houses, taking whatever they found desirable. This was not a fixed tribute demand but an open-ended right of plunder. Ahab called the elders and the people, who agreed this demand was too much — and the refusal set off the war.

QUESTION 2
After Israel's first defeat of his army, what tactical advice did Ben-hadad's servants give him about where and how to fight Israel?
A Israel's gods are gods of the hills; fight them on the plains and we will surely be stronger
B Replace the kings with infantry commanders and attack at night when Israel's watch is weakest
C Besiege Israel's cities until famine weakens them, then strike in the harvest season
D Remove Ahab from the battlefield by targeting him with archers, and the army will scatter
1 Kings 20:23
Ben-hadad's servants reasoned that Israel's gods were gods of the hills, making Israel invincible in the mountains. They urged fighting on the plains where they expected Israel would be weak. They also advised replacing the vassal kings with military commanders. The strategy reflected a pagan worldview of geographically limited deities — a worldview God would decisively refute at Aphek.

QUESTION 3 DETAIL
Who led the first Israelite attack against Ben-hadad's army at Samaria, and at what time of day did the attack begin?
A Ahab's royal guard, attacking at dawn before Ben-hadad's army had assembled
B The 7,000 remaining Israelite soldiers, who charged out at dusk under cover of the setting sun
C Elijah, who returned to lead the army according to the word of the LORD
D The young men of the district commanders — 232 men — who went out at noon
1 Kings 20:14–16
A prophet told Ahab that the young men of the district commanders would lead the attack. There were 232 of them. They went out at noon — while Ben-hadad and his allied kings were getting drunk in their tents. Ahab followed with 7,000 Israelites. The unlikely force, the unusual hour, and the enemy's drunken unpreparedness all highlight that the victory was the LORD's.

QUESTION 4 DETAIL
When Ben-hadad's servants approached Ahab after the second defeat to plead for their master's life, how were they dressed?
A In white linen, barefoot, with palms raised and faces covered in ashes
B In sackcloth around their waists and ropes on their heads
C In royal armor stripped of weapons, bearing gifts of gold as tribute
D In torn garments with their swords surrendered, kneeling at the city gate
1 Kings 20:31–32
The servants proposed going to Ahab wearing sackcloth around their waists and ropes on their heads — garb that signaled complete submission and a plea for mercy. They had heard the kings of Israel were merciful. Ahab accepted Ben-hadad as a "brother" and made a treaty, releasing him. A prophet would soon tell Ahab this mercy came at a catastrophic cost.

QUESTION 5
What verdict did the prophet pronounce against Ahab for releasing Ben-hadad instead of executing him?
A "Your life for his life, and your people for his people"
B "You will not return alive from your next battle against Aram"
C "The kingdom will be torn from your hands before the year is out"
D "No prophet of the LORD will speak to you again until the day of your death"
1 Kings 20:42
The prophet declared: "This is what the LORD says: 'Because you have set free a man I had determined should die, your life will go for his life, your people for his people.'" Ben-hadad was under God's ban (herem) — devoted to destruction. By showing unauthorized mercy, Ahab transferred the death sentence to himself. Ahab went home sullen and angry at the word.

QUESTION 6 DETAIL
After Israel routed the Aramean army at Aphek, what happened to the survivors who fled into the city?
A They surrendered to Ahab's forces and were taken captive to Samaria
B Elijah called fire from the LORD to destroy the city and its remaining defenders
C The city wall collapsed on them, killing 27,000
D Ben-hadad ordered them to fight to the last man inside the city gates
1 Kings 20:29–30
After Israel killed 100,000 Aramean foot soldiers in a single day on the battlefield, the rest fled into the city of Aphek — and the city wall fell on them, killing 27,000. God had promised to defeat the Aramean army to demonstrate He is LORD everywhere, not only in the hills. The wall's collapse underscored that no refuge exists from the God of all the earth.

QUESTION 7
When Ahab offered to buy Naboth's vineyard or trade it for a better one, what reason did Naboth give for refusing?
A The vineyard had been his family's for seven generations and he had sworn to his father never to sell it
B He did not trust that any land Ahab offered in exchange would be comparable in quality
C The vineyard was dedicated to the LORD's use and its grapes could not be given to a king who served Baal
D "The LORD forbid that I should give you the inheritance of my fathers"
1 Kings 21:3
Naboth's refusal was rooted in the Mosaic law's theology of land: ancestral land was a covenantal inheritance (nachalah) given by God, not a commodity to be sold or traded away permanently (cf. Lev. 25:23–28; Num. 36:7). Naboth was not being stubborn — he was being faithful to covenant obligations that even a king had no authority to override.

QUESTION 8
What specific false charge did the two scoundrels bring against Naboth to secure his condemnation and execution?
A They accused him of plotting armed rebellion against the crown
B They testified that Naboth had cursed both God and the king
C They claimed he had withheld the required tithes from the temple and kept them for himself
D They swore he had desecrated the royal seal on letters sent in Ahab's name
1 Kings 21:13
"Naboth has cursed God and the king" — a capital charge under Mosaic law (Lev. 24:16; Ex. 22:28). Jezebel's letters required two witnesses to sit opposite Naboth and testify to this blasphemy, which the law required for capital cases (Deut. 17:6). She weaponized Israel's own legal procedures to destroy an innocent man, then had him stoned. The letter of the law served murder.

QUESTION 9
What specific judgment did Elijah pronounce concerning Jezebel when he confronted Ahab in Naboth's vineyard?
A Dogs will devour Jezebel within the plot of ground at Jezreel
B Jezebel will be handed over to foreign enemies and carried into exile beyond the river
C Jezebel will die in childbirth and her children will be put to the sword
D Fire from heaven will fall on Jezebel's house in the valley of Jezreel
1 Kings 21:23
Elijah declared that dogs would devour Jezebel in the plot of ground at Jezreel. This was fulfilled literally in 2 Kings 9:36–37 after Jehu had her thrown from a window. Elijah's oracle also pronounced that Ahab's blood would be licked up by dogs where Naboth's blood was spilled — place-specific, precise, and ultimately inescapable.

QUESTION 10
After Elijah pronounced full judgment on Ahab's house, why did God tell Elijah He would delay the disaster?
A Elijah interceded for Ahab and God relented out of regard for the prophet's prayer
B Ahab humbled himself — he tore his clothes, fasted, lay in sackcloth, and went about quietly
C Ahab publicly confessed his sin and returned the vineyard to Naboth's sons
D Obadiah and a remnant of faithful Israelites offered sacrifice on Ahab's behalf
1 Kings 21:27–29
When Ahab heard Elijah's words, he tore his clothes, put on sackcloth, fasted, lay in the sackcloth, and went about meekly. The LORD told Elijah: "Have you noticed how Ahab has humbled himself before me? Because he has humbled himself, I will not bring this disaster in his day, but in the days of his son." Even the wicked Ahab found that genuine humility moved God. The delay is grace — not exoneration.
