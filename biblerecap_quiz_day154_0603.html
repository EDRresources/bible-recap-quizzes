QUESTION 1 OF 10 DETAIL
Proverbs 1:7 declares that "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge." What do fools despise?
PROVERBS 1:7
"The fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge; fools despise wisdom and instruction." The pairing is precise: fools are not merely ignorant — they actively despise the very things that would make them wise. This verse is the thesis statement for the entire book of Proverbs.
QUESTION 2 OF 10
In Proverbs 1, Wisdom cries out in the streets but people refuse her. What does she say she will do when their calamity and terror finally come?
PROVERBS 1:26
"I also will laugh at your calamity; I will mock when terror strikes you." This is one of Scripture's most sobering passages on the finality of rejecting wisdom. Wisdom does not beg those who have spent a lifetime scorning her. The opportunity to receive her passes; what remains is the very ruin they chose.
QUESTION 3 OF 10
Proverbs 2 warns about the "forbidden woman" (the adulteress). According to verse 17, what two things does she do that mark the depth of her unfaithfulness?
PROVERBS 2:17
"She forsakes the companion of her youth and forgets the covenant of her God." The description is not merely about sexual infidelity — it is covenant treachery on two levels: against a husband and against God. The "covenant of her God" may refer to the marriage covenant ratified before God, making her sin theological as much as relational.
QUESTION 4 OF 10
Proverbs 2 describes a wicked man who speaks perverse things and has left the paths of uprightness. According to verse 14, what does this man rejoice in?
PROVERBS 2:14
"Who rejoice in doing evil and delight in the perverseness of evil." This is the chilling endpoint of a hardened heart: the wicked man does not merely fall into evil, he takes pleasure in it. Evil has become his delight. Proverbs holds this up as a warning about where unchecked folly ultimately leads.
QUESTION 5 OF 10
Proverbs 3:5 instructs the son to "Trust in the LORD with all your heart." What is the son specifically told not to do?
PROVERBS 3:5
"Trust in the LORD with all your heart, and do not lean on your own understanding." The prohibition against leaning on one's own understanding is the counterpart to total trust in God. Note that verse 7 adds the related prohibition against being "wise in your own eyes" — but that is a separate command. The specific contrast with trusting God is the rejection of self-reliant reasoning.
QUESTION 6 OF 10
Proverbs 3:9–10 instructs the son to honor the LORD with his wealth and with the firstfruits of all his produce. What specific blessing is promised in verse 10?
PROVERBS 3:10
"Then your barns will be filled with plenty, and your vats will be bursting with wine." The promise is agricultural and concrete — overflowing storehouses and wine vats. Proverbs does not spiritualize this away; it presents material blessing as a real fruit of covenant faithfulness, while the rest of Scripture makes clear such blessings are in God's sovereign hand, not a guarantee.
QUESTION 7 OF 10 DETAIL
Proverbs 3:15 says wisdom "is more precious than jewels." How does the verse complete that thought?
PROVERBS 3:15
"She is more precious than jewels, and nothing you desire can compare with her." The other options are real phrases from Proverbs 3 — verses 17, 16, and 18 respectively — making this question a test of which phrase completes verse 15 specifically. The blunt absoluteness of "nothing you desire" makes wisdom the supreme good, eclipsing every other longing.
QUESTION 8 OF 10 DETAIL
According to Proverbs 3:19–20, by what did the LORD found the earth, establish the heavens, and cause the deeps to break open?
PROVERBS 3:19–20
"The LORD by wisdom founded the earth; by understanding he established the heavens; by his knowledge the deeps broke open, and the clouds drop down the dew." Three distinct attributes — wisdom, understanding, knowledge — each given a corresponding act of creation. This passage grounds the pursuit of wisdom in the very structure of the universe: to seek wisdom is to align oneself with the order God built into creation.
QUESTION 9 OF 10
Proverbs 3:11–12 instructs the son not to despise the LORD's discipline. What reason does verse 12 give for why the LORD reproves?
PROVERBS 3:12
"For the LORD reproves him whom he loves, as a father the son in whom he delights." Discipline is reframed here as a mark of love and delight, not punishment or anger. The New Testament picks this up directly in Hebrews 12:5–6, quoting these very verses to comfort suffering believers: God's discipline is fatherly, not punitive.
QUESTION 10 OF 10
Proverbs 2:21–22 closes the chapter with a contrast between the upright and the wicked. What does the text say will happen to each?
PROVERBS 2:21–22
"For the upright will inhabit the land, and those with integrity will remain in it, but the wicked will be cut off from the land, and the treacherous will be uprooted from it." The imagery echoes the covenant land promise — those who walk in wisdom remain; those who reject it are expelled. This is not merely a social observation but a theological claim rooted in God's ordering of the world.
