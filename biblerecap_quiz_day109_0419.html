QUESTION 1 OF 10 DETAIL

Before going to the medium at Endor, Saul sought guidance from the LORD by three different means — but the LORD did not answer him by any of them. What were those three means?

1 Samuel 28:6
1 Samuel 28:6 records that Saul "inquired of the LORD, but the LORD did not answer him, either by dreams or by Urim or by prophets." These three were the recognized channels of divine communication in Israel: visionary dreams (as God had spoken to Samuel), the Urim (oracular lots kept by the priest), and the prophetic word. The silence on all three fronts drives Saul in desperation to seek a medium — a practice he himself had outlawed (v. 3) in obedience to the Mosaic law.
QUESTION 2 OF 10

When Samuel appeared to Saul at Endor, he delivered a precise prophecy about what would happen "tomorrow." Which option most accurately captures what Samuel said?

1 Samuel 28:19
Samuel's prophecy is devastating in its precision: "Tomorrow you and your sons will be with me. The LORD will also give the army of Israel into the hand of the Philistines." The phrase "with me" is a euphemism for death — joining Samuel in the realm of the dead. Option C is wrong because the text specifies that Saul's sons (including Jonathan) would also die. There is no conditional offer of repentance — Samuel's words are a verdict, not a warning.
QUESTION 3 OF 10

Achish, king of Gath, defended David to the other Philistine commanders who wanted him sent away. What specific comparison did Achish use to express his confidence in David?

1 Samuel 29:9
In 1 Samuel 29:9, Achish tells David: "I know that you are as blameless in my sight as an angel of God." This is extraordinary praise from a pagan king — comparing David's trustworthiness to a divine being. The irony is striking: the Philistine king trusts David completely, while Saul (his own king) has spent years trying to kill him. Despite Achish's confidence, the other commanders will not hear of it and insist David be dismissed before battle.
QUESTION 4 OF 10 DETAIL

The Philistine commanders were suspicious of David and quoted a specific song to justify their distrust. What song did they cite, and where had it originated?

1 Samuel 29:5
1 Samuel 29:5 quotes the commanders: "Is not this David, of whom they sang to one another in dances, 'Saul has struck down his thousands, and David his ten thousands'?" This is the same refrain first recorded in 1 Samuel 18:7, where the women of Israel sang it after David killed Goliath — and which first planted jealousy in Saul's heart. Now the song has traveled all the way to Philistia, where it fuels the fear that forces David out of the battle entirely.
QUESTION 5 OF 10

When David and his men returned to Ziklag and found it burned and their families taken captive, the men were so distraught they spoke of stoning David. What does the text specifically say David did at this moment of extreme personal crisis?

1 Samuel 30:6
1 Samuel 30:6 records the turning point: "And David was greatly distressed, for the people spoke of stoning him, because all the people were bitter in soul, each for his sons and daughters. But David strengthened himself in the LORD his God." The weeping until no strength (v. 4) was the response of all the men together; David's specific, personal response — setting him apart as leader — was to turn to God for inner strength. Only after this does he call for Abiathar and the ephod to inquire of the LORD (v. 7).
QUESTION 6 OF 10 DETAIL

During the pursuit of the Amalekites, David's men found an Egyptian slave abandoned in a field and revived him. The text is precise about both his prior condition and what he was given to eat. Which option correctly records both details?

1 Samuel 30:11–12
1 Samuel 30:11–12 specifies the menu with remarkable precision: "They gave him water to drink and food to eat — a piece of a cake of figs and two clusters of raisins. And when he had eaten, his spirit revived, for he had not eaten bread or drunk water for three days and three nights." This small act of compassion proved providential — the revived slave led David's forces directly to the Amalekite camp (v. 15), enabling the full recovery of every captive and all the plunder.
QUESTION 7 OF 10

After defeating the Amalekites, a dispute arose among David's men about sharing the plunder. What rule did David establish, and how does the text describe its lasting significance?

1 Samuel 30:24–25
1 Samuel 30:24–25 records David's ruling: "As his share is who goes down into the battle, so shall his share be who stays by the baggage. They shall share alike." Then: "He made it a statute and a rule for Israel from that day forward to this day." David's counter-argument (v. 23) frames the victory as a gift from God, not earned wages — making equal sharing a theological statement. Those who stayed behind were not cowards but obedient to their orders, and the LORD's gift belongs to the whole community.
QUESTION 8 OF 10

The day after the battle on Mount Gilboa, the Philistines found Saul's body. The text records three distinct actions involving his head, his armor, and his body. Which option correctly lists all three?

1 Samuel 31:9–10
1 Samuel 31:9–10 records three distinct acts: "they cut off his head and stripped off his armor," then spread the news to their idols and their people, and finally "put his armor in the temple of Ashtaroth and fastened his body to the wall of Beth-shan." The grim public display was a declaration of Philistine triumph and divine blessing from their gods. But the men of Jabesh-gilead — the city Saul had once rescued from Nahash the Ammonite (1 Sam 11) — traveled through the night to take down the bodies and give them a proper burial, repaying his former kindness.
QUESTION 9 OF 10 DETAIL

In Psalm 18's dramatic theophany, David describes God physically descending in response to his cry for help. As God "bowed the heavens and came down," what does the psalm specifically say was "under his feet"?

Psalm 18:9
Psalm 18:9 reads: "He bowed the heavens and came down; thick darkness was under his feet." The theophany in Psalm 18 draws on the language of Sinai and the storm — smoke, fire, and coals appear in verse 8, and hailstones with thunder in verses 12–13. But "thick darkness under his feet" is the specific detail of verse 9 alone, echoing the cloud and thick darkness that accompanied God's appearance at Sinai (Deuteronomy 4:11; 5:22). The coals of fire make option B a plausible distractor, but they belong to a different verse.
QUESTION 10 OF 10

Toward the end of Psalm 18, David's praise extends beyond his deliverance from Saul to victories on an international scale. According to verses 43–45, how does David characterize the foreigners who submitted to him?

Psalm 18:43–45
Psalm 18:44–45 reads: "As soon as they heard of me they obeyed me; foreigners came cringing to me. Foreigners lost heart and came trembling out of their fortresses." Submission on the basis of reputation alone — without further battle — points beyond David's personal history to the reach of God's anointed king. Paul quotes verse 49 ("I will praise you among the Gentiles") in Romans 15:9 as a Messianic text, seeing in David's universal praise the foreshadowing of Christ's lordship over all nations. The psalm begins with one man's cry and ends with the whole world bowing.
