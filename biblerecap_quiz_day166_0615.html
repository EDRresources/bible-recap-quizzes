Question 1 of 10
According to Psalm 134, what are the servants of the LORD who minister by night in his house specifically called to do in the sanctuary? DETAIL
Psalm 134:2
"Lift up your hands in the sanctuary and praise the LORD." This brief psalm — the last of the Songs of Ascents — calls the night-watch priests to active praise. The hands lifted in blessing echo the priestly gesture of Numbers 6:24–26, but here it is offered to God, not on behalf of the people.
Question 2 of 10
What specific reason does Psalm 146 give for why we should not put our trust in princes or in human beings?
Psalm 146:3–4
"Do not put your trust in princes, in human beings, who cannot save. When their spirit departs, they return to the ground; on that very day their plans come to nothing." The psalmist's argument is not primarily moral but ontological — human rulers are mortal. Their death severs every plan they made. Only the LORD, who made heaven and earth and "remains faithful forever" (v.6), is a reliable foundation.
Question 3 of 10
Psalm 146 lists many of the LORD's saving acts toward the vulnerable. Which group does the psalm say the LORD actively "frustrates"? DETAIL
Psalm 146:9
"The LORD watches over the foreigner and sustains the fatherless and the widow, but he frustrates the ways of the wicked." The contrast is pointed: God actively sustains the helpless and actively thwarts the wicked. The foreigner appears in the list as one whom God protects, not as someone frustrated — a detail easy to miss when reading quickly.
Question 4 of 10
Psalm 147 describes God sending something to the earth that "runs swiftly." What is it? DETAIL
Psalm 147:15
"He sends his command to the earth; his word runs swiftly." This is immediately followed by natural images — snow, frost, hail, thaw (vv.16–18) — all driven by that same commanding word. The breezes do appear (v.18), but as a result of the word, not as the thing that runs swiftly. The psalm's logic grounds creation's rhythms in God's active, spoken governance.
Question 5 of 10
In Psalm 147, what does the LORD specifically delight in?
Psalm 147:10–11
"His delight is not in the strength of the horse, nor his pleasure in the legs of a warrior; the LORD delights in those who fear him, who put their hope in his unfailing love." Wheat (v.14) and harp (v.7) both appear in Psalm 147, making them plausible distractors — but the psalm is explicit that power and military strength are not what impress God. What delights him is humble, trusting dependence.
Question 6 of 10
What distinguishes Israel from all other nations according to the closing verses of Psalm 147?
Psalm 147:19–20
"He has revealed his word to Jacob, his laws and decrees to Israel. He has done this for no other nation; they do not know his laws." The gate-strengthening (v.13) and the return of exiles (v.2) also appear in this psalm, so they make credible distractors. But the psalm's final note of distinction is specifically about the revelation of Torah — a privilege of knowledge, not merely of geography or military protection.
Question 7 of 10
Psalm 148 calls the sun, moon, and stars to praise the LORD. What reason does the psalm specifically give for this call? DETAIL
Psalm 148:5–6
"Let them praise the name of the LORD, for at his command they were created, and he established them for ever and ever — he issued a decree that will never pass away." The naming of stars (which Psalm 147:4 does mention) is a plausible but wrong answer here. Psalm 148's reason is rooted in creation-by-command and perpetual establishment — the lights praise their Maker because they owe their very existence and permanence to his word.
Question 8 of 10
How does Psalm 148 describe Israel's unique status before the LORD in the psalm's closing verse?
Psalm 148:14
"And he has raised up for his people a horn, the praise of all his faithful servants, of Israel, the people close to his heart." After summoning all creation — heavens, earth, every creature — the psalm ends by identifying the people for whose sake this cosmic praise rings out: Israel, whom God has honored with a raised horn (a sign of strength and salvation) and called his own intimate people.
Question 9 of 10
In Psalm 149, what do the faithful people of God hold in their hands while the praise of God is in their mouths? DETAIL
Psalm 149:6
"May the praise of God be in their mouths and a double-edged sword in their hands." The harp and timbrel appear earlier (v.3) for dancing praise, but in v.6 the image shifts sharply: simultaneous song and sword. The psalm envisions God's people as worshipers who are also instruments of divine judgment — a motif that recurs in Revelation 19, where the Word of God himself wields a sharp sword.
Question 10 of 10
Psalm 150 gives two reasons for praising the LORD in verse 2. What are those two reasons?
Psalm 150:2
"Praise him for his acts of power; praise him for his surpassing greatness." The Psalter's grand doxology keeps its theology simple: praise God for what he does (his mighty deeds) and for who he is (his incomparable greatness). The word/Jacob/laws language is from Psalm 147; the humble/wicked contrast is from Psalms 146–147. Psalm 150 sweeps past all specifics into pure, unrestricted, universal praise.
