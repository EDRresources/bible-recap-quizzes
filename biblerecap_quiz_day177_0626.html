QUESTION 1
What does 1 Kings 16 specifically say about the relationship between Omri's sin and Jeroboam's sin?
A Omri walked in all the ways of Jeroboam and sinned more than all the kings before him
B Omri repented of Jeroboam's sins but introduced new idols from Assyria
C Omri tore down Jeroboam's golden calves but replaced them with temples to Baal
D Omri's sins were counted no worse than Jeroboam's, only more numerous
1 Kings 16:25–26
The text says Omri "sinned more than all those before him" and "followed completely the ways of Jeroboam son of Nebat, committing the same sins Jeroboam had caused Israel to commit." Omri built on Jeroboam's legacy rather than breaking from it.

QUESTION 2
What was the specific cause of Zimri's death after his seven-day reign?
A He was captured by Omri's soldiers and publicly executed in the city square of Tirzah
B He was assassinated by a surviving member of Baasha's household
C He died in battle defending the city gates of Tirzah against Omri
D When he saw the city was taken, he set the palace on fire and burned to death inside it
1 Kings 16:18
When Zimri saw that the city was taken, he "went into the inner part of the royal palace and set the palace on fire around him. So he died." His death was self-destruction rather than defeat in battle or execution.

QUESTION 3
According to 1 Kings 16, what sin of Ahab's specifically exceeded what Jeroboam had done?
A Building a second temple to the LORD in Samaria to compete with Jerusalem
B Marrying Jezebel and then serving and worshiping Baal — which Jeroboam's sin was considered trivial compared to
C Sacrificing his own children as burnt offerings to foreign gods
D Destroying the golden calves at Bethel and Dan and replacing them with an image of Baal
1 Kings 16:31–33
Ahab considered it "trivial" to commit Jeroboam's sins — then went further by marrying Jezebel, serving Baal, building a temple for Baal in Samaria, and making an Asherah pole. His marriage to Jezebel was the gateway to full-scale Baal worship in Israel.

QUESTION 4 DETAIL
What happened to Hiel of Bethel's sons when he rebuilt Jericho?
A They were killed fighting the Canaanites who resisted the rebuilding project
B They were sacrificed by Hiel as offerings at the foundation, as was the custom in Canaan
C He lost his firstborn Abiram when he laid the foundation and his youngest son Segub when he set up the gates
D They were taken captive by enemy nations as divine punishment for violating God's ban on rebuilding
1 Kings 16:34; cf. Joshua 6:26
Hiel rebuilt Jericho "at the cost of his firstborn son Abiram" (at the foundation) and "his youngest son Segub" (at the gates). This fulfilled the curse Joshua had pronounced centuries earlier — a sobering reminder that God's word stands across generations.

QUESTION 5 DETAIL
Where was Elah and what was he doing when Zimri conspired against him?
A He was drinking himself drunk in the house of Arza, the palace administrator at Tirzah
B He was reviewing his troops at the city gate with his military commander Omri
C He was offering sacrifices at the high place outside the city walls

QUESTION 6
How does 2 Chronicles 17 characterize Jehoshaphat's religious orientation when contrasting him with unfaithful kings?
A He restored the Ark of the Covenant to the Temple and rebuilt the altar that Asa had dismantled
B He expelled all the Levites from the high places and consolidated worship at Jerusalem
C He demolished the foreign altars that his father Asa had left standing in the land
D He did not seek the Baals but sought the God of his father and followed his commands rather than the practices of Israel
2 Chronicles 17:3–4
The text says Jehoshaphat "did not consult the Baals but sought the God of his father and followed his commands rather than the practices of Israel." This seeking orientation — directing his heart to God rather than to the idols of the northern kingdom — is the root of his faithfulness.

QUESTION 7
What did Jehoshaphat's officials, Levites, and priests take with them as they traveled throughout the towns of Judah teaching the people?
A The sacred psalms and hymns that David had composed for temple worship
B The Book of the Law of the LORD
C A written royal proclamation from Jehoshaphat declaring the LORD the God of Judah
D The priestly vestments and bronze altar implements as symbols of covenantal authority
2 Chronicles 17:9
"They taught throughout Judah, taking with them the Book of the Law of the LORD; they went around to all the towns of Judah and taught the people." The Word of God itself — not a royal decree or priestly symbol — was the instrument of Jehoshaphat's national reformation.

QUESTION 8
What was the primary effect of Jehoshaphat's reign on the kingdoms surrounding Judah?
A Several surrounding kings sent delegations to Jerusalem seeking military alliances against the northern kingdom
B The Philistines and Arabians declared war to prevent Judah from growing too powerful
C The fear of the LORD fell on all the surrounding kingdoms so that they did not make war against Jehoshaphat
D The surrounding nations paid tribute only after Jehoshaphat defeated them in military campaigns
2 Chronicles 17:10
"The fear of the LORD fell on all the kingdoms of the lands surrounding Judah, so that they did not make war against Jehoshaphat." The Philistines brought gifts and the Arabians brought flocks — but the source of peace was divine fear, not military deterrence. Jehoshaphat didn't fight his way to security; God provided it.

QUESTION 9
In 2 Chronicles 17, whose example does Jehoshaphat explicitly follow as the model for his reign?
A His father David
B His grandfather Solomon
C His father Asa, who first purged Baal worship from Judah
D Moses, through strict enforcement of the Mosaic covenant
2 Chronicles 17:3
"The LORD was with Jehoshaphat because he followed the ways of his father David before him." The Chronicler consistently points back to David as the standard of faithful kingship. Jehoshaphat's biological father was Asa, but his spiritual model was David — hence the phrasing "his father David."

QUESTION 10 DETAIL
Which of the following commanders served Jehoshaphat from the tribe of Benjamin?
A Adnah the commander, stationed with 300,000 men from the house of Judah
B Amasiah son of Zichri, who volunteered himself for the service of the LORD
C Jehohanan the commander, with 280,000 men from Judah
D Eliada, described as a brave soldier, commanding 200,000 men armed with bows and shields
2 Chronicles 17:14–18
The text lists commanders from Judah (Adnah, Jehohanan, and Amasiah) and then turns to Benjamin: "From Benjamin: Eliada, a brave soldier, with 200,000 men armed with bows and shields; next, Jehozabad, with 180,000 men armed for battle." Eliada is the only Benjamin commander described with the honorific "brave soldier."
