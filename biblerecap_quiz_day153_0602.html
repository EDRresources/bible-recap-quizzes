CHALLENGING Song of Solomon 1–8
QUESTION 1 OF 10
In Song of Solomon 1, the beloved says she has neglected her own vineyard while keeping the vineyards of others. What caused her to tend others' vineyards instead of her own?
SONG OF SOLOMON 1:6
The beloved explains: "My mother's sons were angry with me and made me keeper of the vineyards, but my own vineyard I have not kept!" The family conflict behind this detail subtly introduces themes of undeserved hardship and longing running throughout the book.
QUESTION 2 OF 10
In Song of Solomon 2:7, the Shulammite charges the daughters of Jerusalem not to awaken love until it pleases. By what does she charge them?
SONG OF SOLOMON 2:7
"I adjure you, O daughters of Jerusalem, by the gazelles or the does of the field, that you not stir up or awaken love until it pleases." This refrain appears three times in the book (2:7; 3:5; 8:4), using images of graceful, unhurried animals to underscore that love has its proper, unhurried time.
QUESTION 3 OF 10
In Song of Solomon 3, after the Shulammite searches the city and finds her beloved, where does she bring him?
SONG OF SOLOMON 3:4
"Scarcely had I passed them when I found him whom my soul loves. I held him, and would not let him go until I had brought him into my mother's house, and into the chamber of her who conceived me." The detail of the mother's house emphasizes intimacy, belonging, and the covenant context of love.
QUESTION 4 OF 10 DETAIL
Song of Solomon 4 describes the beloved's garden as an orchard filled with fragrant plants. Which of the following is not listed among the plants and spices in that garden description?
SONG OF SOLOMON 4:13–14
The garden includes pomegranates, henna, nard, saffron, calamus, cinnamon, frankincense, myrrh, and aloes — but olive is never mentioned. The lavish spice inventory pictures the beloved's beauty as something richly complex, rare, and costly.
QUESTION 5 OF 10
In Song of Solomon 5, the beloved delays opening the door for her lover, and by the time she rises he has gone. What physical detail in the text shows that he had actually been at the door?
SONG OF SOLOMON 5:4–5
"My beloved put his hand through the latch opening; my heart began to pound for him. I arose to open for my beloved, and my hands dripped with myrrh, my fingers with flowing myrrh, on the handles of the bolt." The myrrh he left on the latch is the only physical trace of his presence — and a poignant reminder of her delay.
QUESTION 6 OF 10 DETAIL
In Song of Solomon 5, the Shulammite describes her beloved to the daughters of Jerusalem. Which of the following correctly pairs a feature with its comparison?
SONG OF SOLOMON 5:12, 14–15
"His eyes are like doves beside streams of water, bathed in milk, mounted like jewels" (v.12). The other options mix up the comparisons: his cheeks are beds of spices (v.13); his arms are rods of gold set with topaz (v.14); his legs are pillars of alabaster set on bases of gold (v.15); his appearance overall is like Lebanon, choice as the cedars.
QUESTION 7 OF 10
In Song of Solomon 6, the daughters of Jerusalem ask where the beloved has gone. What is the Shulammite's answer?
SONG OF SOLOMON 6:2
"My beloved has gone down to his garden, to the beds of spices, to browse in the gardens and to gather lilies." Even in his absence she knows exactly where he belongs — and she declares again, "I am my beloved's and my beloved is mine; he browses among the lilies" (v.3).
QUESTION 8 OF 10 DETAIL
Song of Solomon 6 lists the number of queens, concubines, and virgins in Solomon's court to contrast them with the unique beloved. How many of each are mentioned?
SONG OF SOLOMON 6:8–9
"There are sixty queens and eighty concubines, and virgins without number. But my dove, my perfect one, is the only one." The point is not the numbers themselves but what they prove: amid a multitude, the beloved stands uniquely apart — a picture of the covenant love that prizes the one.
QUESTION 9 OF 10 DETAIL
In Song of Solomon 8, the brothers speak of their "little sister" who has not yet matured. What do they say they will do if she is a wall, versus if she is a door?
SONG OF SOLOMON 8:9
"If she is a wall, we will build on her a battlement of silver, but if she is a door, we will enclose her with boards of cedar." Being a "wall" (chaste, firm) calls for honor; being a "door" (easily opened) calls for protective enclosure. The Shulammite answers that she is indeed a wall (v.10), showing she has guarded her own honor.
QUESTION 10 OF 10
Song of Solomon 8 declares that love cannot be purchased. According to verse 7, if a man offered all the wealth of his house in exchange for love, what would happen to him?
SONG OF SOLOMON 8:7
"If a man offered for love all the wealth of his house, he would be utterly scorned." The verse is not a soft refusal — it is contempt. True love is categorically beyond commerce. This is one of Scripture's most direct statements that grace and covenant love cannot be bought, only given and received.
